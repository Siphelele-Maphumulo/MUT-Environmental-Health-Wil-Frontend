INTRODUCTION

Work Integrated learning (WIL) is a broad term that encompasses various learning opportunities centred on the integration of academic learning and practical application in a chosen work environment (Sattler, 2011). The delivery of WIL by Institutions of higher learning for the students enrolled for the Environmental Health profession is mandated by the Health Professions Council of South Africa (HPCSA). The Health HPCSA requires that this practical engagement be done before students complete their qualifications, without which they are unable to obtain their qualifications. Environmental Health WIL Implementation is highly contextualized and has an impact on the expectations of students, industry, universities and the HPCSA Professional Board.

The Department of Environmental Health (DoEH) at the Mangosuthu University of Technology (MUT) is implementing Work Integrated Learning (WIL) programme which conforms to the Health Professions Council of South Africa (HPCSA) Professional Board for Environmental Health Practitioners and MUT Co-operative Education Department. In this regard, WIL implementation is guided by Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) for the Management of Work Integrated Learning to be conducted by student Environmental Health Practitioners registered with the Health Professions Council of South Africa and MUT Co-operative Education Policy.

Good WIL practice requires institutional vision, educational rigor and strong partnership between university and placement WIL provider. This requires collaboration and careful consideration of the context. At MUT, DoEH has adopted a range of approaches and strategies that integrate theory and practice, within designed Environmental Health curriculum. These includes the following WIL activities:

  • Work placement, this is primarily at the municipalities for the purposes of Municipal Health Services (MHS) as provided for in the National Health Act, 61 of 2003. However, other recognised service providers with specialised services in the Environmental Health field do provide assistance in their areas of speciality as long as those services are aligned with the scope of practice of Environmental Health.
  • Educational Tours and Excursions Programme
  • Community engagement and service learning projects
  • On-campus activities whereby industries come to class to conduct trainings and guest lecturing
  • On-campus activities whereby university together with industries are commemorating environmental health related events

COMMUNICATION TIPS FOR STUDENTS

  • First impression – make eye contact, dress appropriately, punctuality
  • In person, either face-to-face or via phone, rather than by email
  • Language, both written and oral communication; use the language correctly
  • Etiquette, e.g., always begin by asking the respondent if they have a moment to talk
  • Challenge yourself and seek opportunities to talk in front of people and make formal presentations
  • Practice before making a formal presentation
  • Ask if you require assistance or don't understand
  • Formalize documents – letters and reports
  • Proofread documents for spelling, grammar and format (Martin & Hughes, 2009)

GUIDELINES TO STUDENTS

The following are the do's and don'ts of students during WIL placement:

Students should:

  • Be loyal to the placement industry and active in its work
  • Recognize their responsibilities to their employer or client, the public, and fellow employees
  • Disclose any interest that they may have which may impair their professional judgment when dealing with their employer
  • Maintain high standards of professional behaviour during placements. This includes meeting the ethical expectations of the placement industry, especially with regard to confidentiality and discretion in comments to third parties
  • Be punctual to the daily routines of the agencies in which they are placed. Professional practice requires that a framework of routine be set and maintained
  • Meet the standard of dress required by the industry - if unsure of expectations, ask
  • Complete logbook daily and correctly records all activities undertaken during WIL placement

Students should NOT:

  • Conduct themselves in a manner as to prejudice the professional status or reputation of the placement industry
  • Make comparison with, or statements about other members, that are not based on verifiable facts
  • Disclose any confidential information or matter related to their work or the business of their client, without the expressed authority of their employer
  • Entertain or accept any covert reward, profit, or use (for personal gain) any information obtained in their professional capacity
  • Misrepresent their competence nor, without disclosing its limits, undertake work beyond it

GUIDELINES TO INDUSTRY MENTORS

The Department of Environmental Health at Mangosuthu University of Technology would like to thank you for your continued support for Work Integrated Learning (WIL) for students studying for the Environmental Health qualification. We greatly value the time and the impartation of practical professional knowledge to our students.

Ideally, EHP/Industry mentors undertake the following activities during WIL placement:

  • Responsible for the pre-agreed work performed by the student along with the level of practice
  • Negotiate the work duties and learning goals with the academic supervisor and in some cases, with the student
  • Arrange a workplace induction for the student
  • Provide ongoing supervision of the student, preferably through regular timetabled meetings, with written outcomes forming a documentary record of this journey
  • Provide constructive and ongoing feedback on the student's performance in the workplace
  • Provide the student with the opportunity to reconcile workplace theory and practice and the chance to reflect on workplace activities
  • Verify whether learning goals have been met
  • Evaluate and report on student outcomes

BENEFITS

For Students:

  • WIL greatly enhances learning – including inter-disciplinary thinking; the integration of knowledge, skills and competencies; the retention of learning; and the application of subject knowledge
  • WIL affirms career choices – in developing a better understanding of their chosen career, students can realistically evaluate their interest and aptitude for their future work environment; WIL helps to develop a professional identity and introduces the student to positive work values and ethics
  • WIL enhances employability – the student is able to develop a work experience record, and employeers are able to assess potential employees; students are also able to develop a network of future potential employers
  • WIL develops maturity and self-confidence – an understanding of the workplace adds to knowledge of workplace etiquette, interpersonal skills and improved and appropriate communication skills, team work, leadership and co-operation

For University:

  • WIL greatly enhances the curriculum – practical experience lead to more responsive curricula and pedagogy; increasing the validity and relevance of learning programmes
  • WIL results in more motivated learners – improved retention and throughput have been noted when students return to the university
  • WIL helps to build mutually beneficial partnerships – including work placements for teaching staff, which result in up-to-date information from industry; curricula are more closely aligned to work requirements
  • WIL enhances the reputation of the institution – responsiveness to industry needs arise from close linkages and partnerships between institutions and employers

For Industry:

  • WIL provides students a source of potential employees
  • WIL provides an opportunity to influence curricula – new, cutting edge technologies, processes and methods become part of the curriculum
  • WIL is essential in improving the skills base of the country to the benefit of education, training and industry
